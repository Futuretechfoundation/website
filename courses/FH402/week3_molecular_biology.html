Topic 1: Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

Key Concept:

The central dogma of molecular biology describes the flow of genetic information within biological systems: DNA → RNA → Proteins. This fundamental principle underpins most biotechnology applications.

Reading Materials

1.1 Understanding the Central Dogma

The central dogma of molecular biology, first proposed by Francis Crick in 1958, describes the flow of genetic information within a biological system. In its most basic form, it states that DNA makes RNA, which makes protein. This unidirectional flow of information (with some exceptions) is fundamental to understanding how genetic information is utilized in cells.

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) serves as the blueprint or template, storing the genetic information. This information is transcribed into RNA (ribonucleic acid), which then serves as a messenger carrying the instructions from DNA. Finally, the RNA is translated into proteins, which are the functional molecules that carry out most cellular processes.

The Process in Detail:

  1. Replication: DNA makes copies of itself during cell division
  2. Transcription: DNA is used as a template to create messenger RNA (mRNA)
  3. Translation: mRNA serves as a template for protein synthesis at ribosomes

Understanding this process allows biotechnologists to manipulate genetic information at various stages, enabling numerous applications from genetic engineering to medical treatments.

1.2 Exceptions to the Central Dogma

While the central dogma provides a generally accurate model of information flow in biological systems, several important exceptions have been discovered:

  • Reverse Transcription: RNA can be converted back to DNA by reverse transcriptase enzymes, as seen in retroviruses like HIV
  • RNA Replication: Some RNA viruses can replicate their RNA directly without a DNA intermediate
  • Prions: Infectious proteins that can transmit their abnormal folding pattern to normal proteins without nucleic acid involvement

These exceptions have opened up new avenues in biotechnology, such as using reverse transcriptase in creating cDNA libraries from mRNA and developing RNA-based therapies.

Topic 2: DNA Structure and Function

2.1 DNA Structure

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is a molecule that carries the genetic instructions used in the growth, development, functioning, and reproduction of all known living organisms. The DNA molecule consists of two strands that wrap around each other to form a double helix structure, resembling a twisted ladder.

Each strand of DNA is composed of nucleotides, which include:

  • A phosphate group
  • A deoxyribose sugar molecule
  • One of four nitrogenous bases: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G), or Cytosine (C)

The two strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between complementary base pairs: A pairs with T, and G pairs with C. This complementary base pairing is crucial for DNA replication and transcription.

Key Features of DNA Structure:

  • Double-helix structure with antiparallel strands
  • Complementary base pairing (A-T, G-C)
  • Sugar-phosphate backbone on the exterior
  • Nitrogenous bases oriented toward the interior
  • Major and minor grooves along the helix

2.2 DNA Replication

DNA replication is the process by which DNA makes a copy of itself during cell division. This process is semi-conservative, meaning that each strand of the original DNA molecule serves as a template for the synthesis of a new complementary strand.

The major steps in DNA replication include:

  1. Initiation: The DNA double helix unwinds at specific points called origins of replication, where the enzyme helicase breaks the hydrogen bonds between the base pairs.
  2. Elongation: DNA polymerase adds complementary nucleotides to each template strand in the 5' to 3' direction. Due to the antiparallel nature of DNA, one strand (the leading strand) is synthesized continuously, while the other (the lagging strand) is synthesized in short fragments called Okazaki fragments.
  3. Termination: When replication forks meet, synthesis stops, and the new DNA molecules are complete.

DNA replication is remarkably accurate, with error rates as low as one mistake per billion base pairs. This accuracy is achieved through the proofreading capabilities of DNA polymerase and various repair mechanisms.

2.3 DNA Sequencing Technologies

DNA sequencing is the process of determining the precise order of nucleotides within a DNA molecule. Modern DNA sequencing technologies have revolutionized biotechnology by enabling rapid, cost-effective genome sequencing.

First-Generation Sequencing:

  • Sanger Sequencing: Uses dideoxynucleotides to terminate DNA synthesis at specific nucleotides, followed by capillary electrophoresis to separate fragments by size.

Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS):

  • Illumina Sequencing: Uses bridge amplification and sequencing by synthesis with fluorescently labeled nucleotides.
  • Ion Torrent Sequencing: Detects hydrogen ions released during nucleotide incorporation.
  • 454 Pyrosequencing: Detects pyrophosphate release during nucleotide incorporation.

Third-Generation Sequencing:

  • Oxford Nanopore Sequencing: Measures changes in electrical conductivity as DNA passes through a protein nanopore.
  • PacBio SMRT (Single Molecule Real-Time) Sequencing: Observes DNA polymerase in real-time as it incorporates nucleotides.

These technologies have enabled numerous applications, including:

  • Whole genome sequencing
  • Targeted gene sequencing
  • RNA sequencing (transcriptomics)
  • Metagenomics
  • Epigenomics

Topic 3: RNA Structure and Function

3.1 RNA Structure

RNA (ribonucleic acid) differs from DNA in several key ways:

  • RNA is typically single-stranded (though it can form complex secondary structures)
  • RNA contains ribose sugar (with an OH group on the 2' carbon) instead of deoxyribose
  • RNA uses the base uracil (U) instead of thymine (T)
  • RNA is generally less stable and more reactive than DNA

Despite being single-stranded, RNA can fold into complex three-dimensional structures through intramolecular base pairing, which is crucial for its various functions.

3.2 Types of RNA and Their Functions

Several types of RNA play crucial roles in cellular processes:

Messenger RNA (mRNA):

  • Carries genetic information from DNA to ribosomes
  • Serves as a template for protein synthesis
  • Contains coding regions (exons) separated by non-coding regions (introns) in eukaryotes
  • Undergoes post-transcriptional modifications including 5' capping, 3' polyadenylation, and splicing

Transfer RNA (tRNA):

  • Transports amino acids to ribosomes during translation
  • Has a cloverleaf secondary structure and L-shaped tertiary structure
  • Contains an anticodon loop that base-pairs with mRNA codons
  • Carries specific amino acids linked to its 3' end

Ribosomal RNA (rRNA):

  • Major component of ribosomes (60-80% by weight)
  • Catalyzes peptide bond formation (ribozyme activity)
  • Forms the structural and functional core of ribosomes

Non-coding RNAs (ncRNAs):

  • microRNAs (miRNAs): Regulate gene expression by binding to target mRNAs
  • Small interfering RNAs (siRNAs): Involved in RNA interference (RNAi) pathway
  • Long non-coding RNAs (lncRNAs): Regulate transcription and post-transcriptional processes
  • Small nuclear RNAs (snRNAs): Essential components of spliceosomes
  • CRISPR RNAs (crRNAs): Guide Cas nucleases to specific DNA sequences in CRISPR-Cas systems

3.3 Transcription: DNA to RNA

Transcription is the process by which the information in DNA is copied into RNA. This process occurs in three main stages:

1. Initiation:

  • RNA polymerase binds to the promoter region of DNA
  • In eukaryotes, transcription factors assist RNA polymerase in recognizing promoters
  • The DNA double helix unwinds to form a transcription bubble

2. Elongation:

  • RNA polymerase moves along the template strand of DNA in the 3' to 5' direction
  • Complementary ribonucleotides are added to the growing RNA strand in the 5' to 3' direction
  • The RNA strand temporarily forms a DNA-RNA hybrid with the template strand

3. Termination:

  • In prokaryotes, termination occurs either through intrinsic termination (hairpin formation) or rho-dependent termination
  • In eukaryotes, termination involves recognition of specific sequences and cleavage of the transcript

In eukaryotic cells, the primary RNA transcript undergoes processing to form mature mRNA:

  • Addition of a 5' cap (7-methylguanosine)
  • Addition of a 3' poly(A) tail
  • Splicing to remove introns and join exons
  • Alternative splicing can generate multiple mRNA variants from a single gene

Quick Check

3. Which of the following is NOT a type of RNA?

Correct Answer: c) dRNA

dRNA is not a recognized type of RNA. The main types of RNA include messenger RNA (mRNA), transfer RNA (tRNA), ribosomal RNA (rRNA), and various non-coding RNAs.

Topic 4: Protein Synthesis and Structure

4.1 Translation: RNA to Protein

Translation is the process by which the nucleotide sequence of mRNA is used to direct protein synthesis. This process occurs in three main stages:

1. Initiation:

  • In prokaryotes, the small ribosomal subunit binds to the Shine-Dalgarno sequence on mRNA
  • In eukaryotes, the small ribosomal subunit binds to the 5' cap and scans for the start codon (AUG)
  • The initiator tRNA carrying methionine binds to the start codon
  • The large ribosomal subunit joins to form the complete ribosome

2. Elongation:

  • The ribosome has three sites: A (aminoacyl), P (peptidyl), and E (exit)
  • Charged tRNAs enter the A site, where their anticodons base-pair with mRNA codons
  • A peptide bond forms between the amino acid in the P site and the amino acid in the A site
  • The ribosome translocates, moving mRNA and tRNAs through the sites
  • The process repeats, adding amino acids to the growing polypeptide chain

3. Termination:

  • When a stop codon (UAA, UAG, or UGA) enters the A site, release factors bind instead of tRNA
  • The release factors trigger hydrolysis of the bond between the polypeptide and the tRNA
  • The completed polypeptide is released
  • The ribosomal subunits dissociate

4.2 The Genetic Code

The genetic code is the set of rules by which nucleotide sequences in DNA and RNA are translated into proteins. Key features include:

  • Triplet Code: Three consecutive nucleotides (a codon) specify one amino acid
  • Degeneracy: Most amino acids are encoded by multiple codons
  • Universality: The code is largely the same in all organisms, with some exceptions
  • Non-overlapping: Each nucleotide belongs to only one codon
  • Start and Stop Signals: AUG serves as the start codon, while UAA, UAG, and UGA serve as stop codons

The Standard Genetic Code:

With 4 possible bases at each of 3 positions, there are 64 possible codons (4³ = 64). These encode 20 standard amino acids and the stop signals.

Examples:

  • UUU, UUC: Phenylalanine (Phe, F)
  • AUG: Methionine (Met, M) and Start signal
  • UAA, UAG, UGA: Stop signals

4.3 Protein Structure and Function

Proteins are complex molecules that perform a vast array of functions in the body. Their structure can be described at four levels:

1. Primary Structure:

  • The linear sequence of amino acids linked by peptide bonds
  • Determined directly by the genetic code

2. Secondary Structure:

  • Local folding patterns stabilized by hydrogen bonds between backbone atoms
  • Common motifs include alpha helices and beta sheets

3. Tertiary Structure:

  • Three-dimensional arrangement of the entire polypeptide chain
  • Stabilized by various interactions including hydrophobic interactions, hydrogen bonds, ionic bonds, and disulfide bridges

4. Quaternary Structure:

  • The arrangement of multiple protein subunits into a functional complex
  • Not all proteins have quaternary structure
  • Examples include hemoglobin (four subunits) and antibodies

Protein folding is crucial for function, and misfolding can lead to diseases such as Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and prion diseases. Chaperone proteins assist in proper folding in the cell.

Proteins perform numerous functions in the body, including:

  • Enzymes: Catalyze biochemical reactions
  • Structural proteins: Provide cellular and tissue structure (e.g., collagen)
  • Transport proteins: Move molecules across membranes or through fluids (e.g., hemoglobin)
  • Signaling proteins: Transmit information between cells (e.g., hormones, cytokines)
  • Receptor proteins: Detect signals and initiate cellular responses
  • Antibodies: Recognize and neutralize foreign substances
  • Motor proteins: Generate mechanical force for movement (e.g., myosin)

Topic 5: Gene Regulation

5.1 Prokaryotic Gene Regulation

In prokaryotes, gene regulation primarily occurs at the transcriptional level and often involves operons—clusters of genes transcribed as a single unit. Key regulatory mechanisms include:

Operons:

  • Consist of structural genes, a promoter, and an operator
  • May include a regulator gene that produces a repressor or activator protein

Negative Regulation:

  • Repressor proteins bind to operators to prevent transcription
  • Example: The lac operon in E. coli, where the repressor prevents transcription in the absence of lactose

Positive Regulation:

  • Activator proteins bind to the promoter or nearby regions to enhance transcription
  • Example: The catabolite activator protein (CAP) enhances transcription of the lac operon in the presence of glucose

Attenuation:

  • Regulation of gene expression based on the rate of translation
  • Example: The trp operon, where transcription is terminated prematurely when tryptophan is abundant

5.2 Eukaryotic Gene Regulation

Eukaryotic gene regulation is more complex and occurs at multiple levels:

Chromatin Structure:

  • DNA is wrapped around histone proteins to form nucleosomes
  • Chromatin remodeling affects gene accessibility
  • Heterochromatin is tightly packed and transcriptionally inactive
  • Euchromatin is less condensed and transcriptionally active

Epigenetic Modifications:

  • DNA methylation typically represses gene expression
  • Histone modifications can either activate or repress transcription
  • Acetylation generally activates transcription by loosening chromatin
  • Methylation can activate or repress depending on the specific amino acid residue

Transcriptional Regulation:

  • Promoter regions contain a TATA box and other regulatory elements
  • Enhancers are distant DNA sequences that increase transcription
  • Silencers are distant DNA sequences that decrease transcription
  • Transcription factors bind to these regulatory elements
  • General transcription factors form the pre-initiation complex
  • Specific transcription factors regulate particular genes

Post-transcriptional Regulation:

  • Alternative splicing generates multiple mRNAs from a single pre-mRNA
  • mRNA stability affects how long a message persists in the cell
  • RNA interference (RNAi) involves small RNAs that target specific mRNAs for degradation
  • microRNAs (miRNAs) bind to target mRNAs and repress translation

Translational Regulation:

  • Initiation factors control the rate of translation initiation
  • Upstream open reading frames (uORFs) can inhibit translation of the main ORF
  • RNA-binding proteins can block or enhance translation

Post-translational Regulation:

  • Protein modifications such as phosphorylation, glycosylation, and ubiquitination
  • Protein degradation through the ubiquitin-proteasome system
  • Protein localization to specific cellular compartments

5.3 Gene Regulation in Biotechnology

Understanding gene regulation is crucial for many biotechnology applications:

Inducible Expression Systems:

  • Tetracycline-inducible systems allow gene expression to be turned on by adding tetracycline
  • IPTG-inducible systems based on the lac operon are widely used in laboratories

Gene Silencing Technologies:

  • RNA interference (RNAi) using small interfering RNAs (siRNAs)
  • CRISPR interference (CRISPRi) using catalytically inactive Cas9

Synthetic Biology:

  • Design of synthetic gene circuits with specific regulatory properties
  • Creation of genetic toggle switches and oscillators
  • Development of biosensors that respond to specific environmental signals

Quick Check

5. Which of the following is NOT a level of gene regulation in eukaryotes?

Correct Answer: b) Operon regulation

Operons are a feature of prokaryotic gene regulation, not eukaryotic. Eukaryotic genes are generally not organized into operons. Instead, each gene typically has its own promoter and regulatory elements.

Topic 6: Molecular Biology Techniques

6.1 Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

PCR is a technique used to amplify specific DNA sequences, generating millions of copies from a small amount of starting material. The process involves three main steps repeated in cycles:

  1. Denaturation: DNA is heated to 94-98°C to separate the double strands
  2. Annealing: Temperature is lowered to 50-65°C to allow primers to bind to the target regions
  3. Extension: Temperature is raised to 72°C for DNA polymerase to synthesize new DNA strands

Key components of PCR include:

  • DNA template: The DNA containing the target sequence
  • Primers: Short DNA oligonucleotides that flank the target sequence
  • DNA polymerase: Typically Taq polymerase, which is heat-stable
  • dNTPs: The building blocks for new DNA strands
  • Buffer and salts: Provide optimal conditions for the polymerase

Variations of PCR include:

  • Quantitative PCR (qPCR): Measures the amount of DNA in real-time using fluorescent dyes
  • Reverse Transcription PCR (RT-PCR): Converts RNA to cDNA before amplification
  • Multiplex PCR: Amplifies multiple targets simultaneously
  • Nested PCR: Uses two sets of primers for increased specificity
  • Digital PCR (dPCR): Provides absolute quantification by partitioning samples into many reactions

6.2 Cloning and Recombinant DNA Technology

Cloning involves inserting a DNA fragment of interest into a vector to create a recombinant DNA molecule, which can then be replicated in a host organism. The process typically includes:

  1. DNA Isolation: Extract DNA containing the gene of interest
  2. Restriction Digest: Cut the DNA and vector with restriction enzymes
  3. Ligation: Join the DNA fragment and vector using DNA ligase
  4. Transformation: Introduce the recombinant DNA into host cells
  5. Selection: Identify and grow cells containing the recombinant DNA
  6. Screening: Verify that the clone contains the correct insert

Common vectors include:

  • Plasmids: Small circular DNA molecules used in bacteria
  • Bacteriophages: Viruses that infect bacteria
  • Cosmids: Plasmids with phage packaging signals
  • Bacterial Artificial Chromosomes (BACs): Can carry large inserts (100-300 kb)
  • Yeast Artificial Chromosomes (YACs): Can carry very large inserts (up to 2 Mb)

Modern cloning often uses techniques such as Gibson Assembly, Golden Gate Assembly, or TOPO cloning, which do not rely on restriction enzymes.

6.3 Gel Electrophoresis and Blotting Techniques

Gel Electrophoresis:

Gel electrophoresis separates DNA, RNA, or proteins based on size by applying an electric field to move the negatively charged molecules through a gel matrix.

  • Agarose gels: Used for DNA and larger RNA fragments
  • Polyacrylamide gels: Used for proteins and smaller DNA/RNA fragments

Blotting Techniques:

  • Southern Blot: Detects specific DNA sequences
    1. DNA is digested with restriction enzymes and separated by gel electrophoresis
    2. DNA is transferred to a membrane
    3. Labeled probes hybridize to complementary sequences
    4. Hybridization is detected through autoradiography or chemiluminescence
  • Northern Blot: Similar to Southern blot but detects RNA sequences
  • Western Blot: Detects specific proteins
    1. Proteins are separated by SDS-PAGE
    2. Proteins are transferred to a membrane
    3. Primary antibodies bind to target proteins
    4. Secondary antibodies conjugated to enzymes or fluorophores bind to primary antibodies
    5. Signal is detected through various methods

6.4 Next-Generation Molecular Biology Techniques

CRISPR-Cas9 Genome Editing:

  • Uses a guide RNA to direct Cas9 nuclease to specific DNA sequences
  • Creates double-strand breaks that can be repaired by non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) or homology-directed repair (HDR)
  • Applications include gene knockout, gene correction, and gene regulation

Single-Cell Technologies:

  • Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq): Analyzes gene expression in individual cells
  • Single-cell ATAC-seq: Maps open chromatin regions in individual cells
  • Single-cell proteomics: Measures protein levels in individual cells

Spatial Transcriptomics:

  • Maps gene expression within tissues while preserving spatial information
  • Examples include in situ sequencing, MERFISH, and Visium spatial gene expression

Long-Read Sequencing:

  • Generates reads of tens to hundreds of kilobases
  • Used for de novo genome assembly, detecting structural variants, and analyzing complex regions
  • Technologies include Oxford Nanopore and PacBio SMRT sequencing

Quick Check

6. Which technique would be most appropriate for measuring the expression levels of thousands of genes simultaneously in a tissue sample?

Correct Answer: c) RNA sequencing (RNA-seq)

RNA sequencing allows for the comprehensive analysis of the entire transcriptome, measuring the expression levels of thousands of genes simultaneously. Western blotting detects specific proteins, PCR amplifies specific DNA sequences, and gel electrophoresis separates molecules by size but does not provide expression data.

Week 3 Review Quiz

Test your understanding of this week's material with the following quiz. This is for self-assessment only and does not count toward your grade.

1. What is the primary role of mRNA in protein synthesis?

Correct Answer: b) To carry genetic information from DNA to ribosomes

mRNA (messenger RNA) transcribes the genetic information from DNA and carries it to the ribosomes, where it serves as a template for protein synthesis.

2. Which of the following is NOT a component of a nucleotide?

Correct Answer: d) Amino acid

Nucleotides consist of a phosphate group, a pentose sugar (ribose in RNA, deoxyribose in DNA), and a nitrogenous base (A, T/U, G, C). Amino acids are the building blocks of proteins, not nucleotides.

3. In DNA replication, why are Okazaki fragments formed?

Correct Answer: a) Because DNA polymerase can only add nucleotides to the 3' end of a growing strand

DNA polymerase can only synthesize DNA in the 5' to 3' direction. Since the two strands of DNA are antiparallel and the replication fork moves in one direction, one strand (the lagging strand) must be synthesized in short fragments (Okazaki fragments) in the opposite direction of the fork movement.

5. Which of the following is a difference between prokaryotic and eukaryotic gene expression?

Correct Answer: b) Prokaryotic mRNA can be translated while still being transcribed

In prokaryotes, transcription and translation can occur simultaneously since they lack a nuclear membrane. In eukaryotes, transcription occurs in the nucleus and translation occurs in the cytoplasm after the mRNA has been processed and exported.

6. What is the function of a promoter in gene expression?

Correct Answer: b) It provides the binding site for RNA polymerase to initiate transcription

A promoter is a sequence of DNA located upstream of a gene that provides a binding site for RNA polymerase and transcription factors, facilitating the initiation of transcription.

7. Which molecular biology technique uses a heat-stable DNA polymerase to amplify specific DNA sequences?

Correct Answer: c) Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR)

PCR uses a heat-stable DNA polymerase (typically Taq polymerase) to amplify specific DNA sequences through repeated cycles of denaturation, annealing, and extension.

8. What type of chemical bond holds amino acids together in a protein?

Correct Answer: c) Peptide bond

Peptide bonds are covalent bonds formed between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of another, linking amino acids together in a protein's primary structure.

9. Which of the following is a post-transcriptional modification in eukaryotes?

Correct Answer: a) Addition of a poly-A tail

The addition of a poly-A tail is a post-transcriptional modification that occurs in eukaryotes. Other post-transcriptional modifications include 5' capping and splicing to remove introns.

10. What is the role of CRISPR-Cas9 in genome editing?

Correct Answer: c) It creates targeted double-strand breaks in DNA

CRISPR-Cas9 is a genome editing system where the Cas9 protein creates precise double-strand breaks in DNA at locations specified by a guide RNA. These breaks can then be repaired by the cell's machinery, allowing for gene modification.
