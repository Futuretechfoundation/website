Week 7: Policy & Ethics in Advanced Biotechnology

Module Overview

Welcome to Week 7 of FH402! This week, we focus on the complex policy frameworks and ethical considerations surrounding cutting-edge biotechnology applications. As technology advances rapidly, our regulatory frameworks and ethical thinking must evolve to keep pace. This module provides a comprehensive examination of current policies governing biotechnology and explores the ethical implications of next-generation biotech applications.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this week, students will be able to:

  • Analyze existing policy frameworks governing biotechnology across different jurisdictions
  • Evaluate the ethical implications of emerging biotech applications including CRISPR, synthetic biology, and AI-driven biotech
  • Formulate evidence-based positions on controversial bioethical issues
  • Identify regulatory gaps that may pose risks to health, environment, or social equity
  • Apply ethical frameworks to real-world case studies in advanced biotechnology

Ethical Frameworks in Biotechnology

Understanding different ethical frameworks helps us navigate complex decisions in biotechnology development and application:

Utilitarianism

Core principle: Maximize benefit, minimize harm across populations

Application: Often used to justify innovations that may carry risks but promise significant benefits, such as gene therapy for previously untreatable conditions.

Limitation: May undervalue individual rights when pursuing "greater good"

Deontological Ethics

Core principle: Actions must align with universal moral duties and obligations

Application: Emphasizes informed consent, human dignity, and refraining from using humans solely as means to ends.

Limitation: May be too rigid when facing novel ethical scenarios created by new technologies

Biocentrism

Core principle: All living beings have inherent value and moral standing

Application: Extends ethical consideration beyond humans to ecosystems when evaluating genetic engineering of non-human species.

Limitation: Difficulty in practical application when balancing competing interests across species

Justice and Equity

Core principle: Fair distribution of benefits and burdens across society

Application: Questions of access to expensive therapies, global North-South divides in technology access, and addressing historical injustices.

Limitation: Competing definitions of what constitutes "fairness" in distribution

Critical Ethical Issues in Advanced Biotechnology

1. Germline Editing and Human Enhancement

The ability to make heritable genetic modifications raises profound questions about human identity, future generations' autonomy, and potential societal inequalities. While therapeutic applications may prevent serious diseases, enhancements for non-medical purposes remain highly controversial.

Case Study: CRISPR Human Trials

Examine the evolution of regulatory approaches following the 2018 announcement of CRISPR-edited babies in China. Compare the international responses, including the WHO's governance framework and national moratoria on clinical applications of germline editing.

2. Synthetic Biology and Biosecurity

As DNA synthesis becomes more accessible and AI-driven design tools advance, concerns grow about dual-use research and biosecurity risks. The democratization of biotechnology creates tension between innovation and necessary safeguards against misuse.

Case Study: DIYbio Movement

Analyze the community biology movement's self-governance mechanisms and their relationship with traditional regulatory frameworks. Consider the benefits of democratized science against biosafety and biosecurity concerns.

3. Data Privacy and Genetic Information

The proliferation of genetic testing, biobanks, and health data aggregation creates complex privacy challenges. Questions of consent, data ownership, and protection against genetic discrimination remain inadequately addressed in many jurisdictions.

Case Study: Population Biobanks

Evaluate the ethical frameworks established by major biobank initiatives like the UK Biobank and All of Us Research Program. Compare their approaches to informed consent, data sharing, and benefit distribution.

4. Environmental Impact and Ecological Engineering

Applications like gene drives for pest control or engineered microbiomes for environmental remediation pose questions about ecological disruption, unintended consequences, and humanity's relationship with natural systems.

Case Study: Gene Drive Technology

Examine the governance challenges of gene drive technologies designed to suppress disease vectors. Consider the ethical implications of releasing self-perpetuating genetic modifications into wild populations without full ecosystem impact understanding.

5. Global Justice and Access

Advanced biotechnologies often develop within a context of global inequality. Consideration must be given to ensuring equitable access to benefits, preventing exploitation of vulnerable populations, and respecting indigenous knowledge and genetic resources.

Case Study: mRNA Vaccine Distribution

Analyze the patterns of access to mRNA vaccines during the COVID-19 pandemic and the ethical frameworks that guided or failed to guide global distribution. Consider proposals for more equitable technology transfer and capacity building.

Current Regulatory Gaps

Despite extensive regulatory frameworks, several areas remain inadequately addressed:

  • AI-Biotech Integration: Current regulations struggle to keep pace with AI-driven drug discovery, protein design, and synthetic biology applications
  • International Harmonization: Regulatory divergence creates challenges for global research collaboration and technology deployment
  • Emerging Technologies: Novel approaches like organoid research, neurological interfaces, and biological computing outpace existing regulatory categories
  • Participatory Governance: Limited mechanisms exist for broader stakeholder and public engagement in biotechnology governance
